Aldous Huxley-:-Aldous Huxley (1894-1963) was a British writer renowned for his insightful and often dystopian novels and essays. Born into a family of intellectuals (his grandfather was Thomas Henry Huxley, a prominent biologist), Aldous Huxley explored a wide range of interests from an early age. He studied English literature at Balliol College, Oxford, but his education was interrupted by a rare eye illness that left him partially blind for much of his life. Huxley's literary career began in the 1920s with novels such as 'Crome Yellow' (1921) and 'Antic Hay' (1923), which satirized British society and its intellectual elites. He gained widespread acclaim with 'Brave New World' (1932), a novel set in a future world where individuality is suppressed in a scientifically engineered society. This work remains one of the defining classics of dystopian literature. Throughout his career, Huxley wrote on a variety of subjects including philosophy, spirituality, and social criticism. He explored the potential of psychedelics in his later years, particularly after experimenting with mescaline, which he detailed in 'The Doors of Perception' (1954). This exploration influenced his later works, where he delved into themes of consciousness expansion and human potential. Aldous Huxley's writing style combined wit, intellectual depth, and a keen awareness of societal issues. He continued to write prolifically until his death in 1963, leaving a lasting legacy in literature and thought, influencing generations of readers with his provocative ideas and visionary storytelling.-:-['Brave New World']
George Orwell-:-George Orwell (1903-1950), born Eric Arthur Blair, was a British writer, journalist, and critic known for his sharp social commentary and satirical works. He is best known for his dystopian novel '1984' and the allegorical novella 'Animal Farm', both of which critique totalitarian regimes and social injustice. Orwell's experiences as a colonial police officer in Burma and his involvement in the Spanish Civil War profoundly influenced his writing, providing firsthand insights into the mechanics of power and oppression. His clear, direct prose and keen observations on political and social issues have cemented his status as one of the most important writers of the 20th century. Orwell's works continue to resonate with readers, offering timeless critiques of authoritarianism and advocacy for democratic socialism.-:-['Animal Farm', '1984', 'Homage to Catalonia', 'Shooting an Elephant', 'The Spike', 'Why I Write']
William Faulkner-:-William Faulkner (1897-1962) was an American writer and Nobel Prize laureate, widely regarded as one of the greatest novelists of the 20th century. Born in Mississippi, Faulkner's work is deeply rooted in the Southern United States, and he is known for his complex narratives and innovative literary techniques. His most famous novels include 'The Sound and the Fury', 'As I Lay Dying', and 'Light in August', which explore themes of identity, race, and the human condition. Faulkner's intricate prose and use of stream of consciousness challenged conventional storytelling, earning him critical acclaim and a lasting influence on modern literature. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1949 for his powerful and artistically unique contribution to contemporary American fiction.-:-['Sanctuary', 'If I Forget Thee, Jerusalem', 'Requiem for a Nun']
Ernest Hemingway-:-Ernest Hemingway (1899-1961) was an American novelist, short story writer, and journalist, known for his succinct and economical writing style. Born in Oak Park, Illinois, Hemingway served in World War I and worked as a journalist before turning to fiction. His experiences shaped much of his writing, which often explores themes of courage, loss, and the human condition. Notable works include 'The Old Man and the Sea', 'A Farewell to Arms', and 'The Sun Also Rises'. Hemingway's distinctive style and his portrayal of stoic, resilient characters have had a profound impact on 20th-century literature. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1954 and remains a key figure in modern American literature.-:-['The Sun Also Rises', 'Men Without Women', 'The Old Man and the Sea', 'Green Hills of Africa', 'In Our Time']
Oscar Wilde-:-Oscar Wilde (1854-1900) was an Irish playwright, novelist, and poet, celebrated for his wit, flamboyant style, and biting social commentary. Educated at Trinity College, Dublin, and later at Oxford, Wilde became a prominent figure in London society. His most famous works include the novel 'The Picture of Dorian Gray' and the plays 'The Importance of Being Earnest' and 'An Ideal Husband'. Wilde's sharp, epigrammatic dialogue and exploration of aestheticism and moral duality made him a defining voice of the late Victorian era. Despite his professional success, Wilde's life ended in scandal and imprisonment for "gross indecency" due to his homosexuality. His work continues to be celebrated for its literary brilliance and social critique.-:-['The Picture of Dorian Gray', 'The Ballad of Reading Gaol', 'A Woman of No Importance', 'A Florentine Tragedy', 'The Critic as Artist', 'The Soul of Man Under Socialism', 'Lord Arthur Savile's Crime and Other Stories']
Mark Twain-:-Mark Twain (1835-1910), born Samuel Langhorne Clemens, was an American author and humorist, best known for his novels 'The Adventures of Tom Sawyer' and 'The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn'. Twain's writing is noted for its vivid depictions of 19th-century American life, his keen wit, and his critique of societal norms. Born in Missouri, Twain worked as a riverboat pilot, journalist, and lecturer before achieving literary fame. His works often tackle themes of race, identity, and morality, and his use of vernacular speech brought authenticity to his characters and settings. Twain is celebrated as one of the greatest American writers, and his influence on literature and humor is still felt today.-:-['The Adventures of Tom Sawyer', 'The Innocents Abroad', 'Letters from the Earth', 'A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court', 'Roughing It']
Thomas Hardy-:-Thomas Hardy (1840-1928) was an English novelist and poet, known for his portrayal of the tragic lives and struggles of rural characters. Born in Dorset, Hardy trained as an architect before turning to writing. His novels, such as 'Tess of the d'Urbervilles', 'Far from the Madding Crowd', and 'Jude the Obscure', often explore themes of fate, social criticism, and the harsh realities of Victorian society. Hardy's use of the Wessex landscape as a fictional setting for his works adds a unique depth to his storytelling. Though controversial during his lifetime for his critical portrayal of society, Hardy's literary legacy has endured, and he is regarded as one of the greatest English writers.-:-['Life's Little Ironies', 'A Pair of Blue Eyes', 'Desperate Remedies', 'A Laodicean', 'The Well-Beloved', 'Wessex Poems']
Emily Brontë-:-Emily Brontë (1818-1848) was an English novelist and poet, best known for her only novel, 'Wuthering Heights', a cornerstone of English literature. Born in Yorkshire, Brontë was part of a literary family, which included her sisters Charlotte and Anne. Her writing is characterized by its emotional intensity, complex characters, and the depiction of the wild Yorkshire moors. 'Wuthering Heights', published under the pseudonym Ellis Bell, was initially controversial for its dark and brooding narrative but has since become a classic. Emily Brontë's poetry and prose continue to be celebrated for their passionate and imaginative qualities, securing her place as one of the great writers of the 19th century.-:-['Hope', 'Fluctuations', 'The Prisoner']
J.R.R. Tolkien-:-J.R.R. Tolkien (1892-1973) was an English writer, poet, philologist, and academic, best known for his high fantasy works 'The Hobbit' and 'The Lord of the Rings'. Born in Bloemfontein, South Africa, Tolkien's early life and academic career in philology heavily influenced his creation of intricate languages and rich, immersive worlds. His literary work is characterized by its deep mythological and historical roots, creating a vast, interconnected legendarium. Tolkien's stories of Middle-earth have had a profound impact on modern fantasy literature, establishing many of the conventions and tropes still used today. He remains a towering figure in fantasy literature, beloved by readers worldwide for his imaginative storytelling and detailed world-building.-:-['The Book of Lost Tales', 'Farmer Giles of Ham', 'The Lost Road and Other Writings']
Fyodor Dostoevsky-:-Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821-1881) was a Russian novelist, short story writer, and essayist, acclaimed for his profound exploration of human psychology and existential themes. Born in Moscow, Dostoevsky experienced significant hardship, including a mock execution and years of penal servitude in Siberia, which deeply influenced his writing. His major works, including 'Crime and Punishment', 'The Brothers Karamazov', and 'Notes from Underground', examine the complexities of morality, faith, and the human condition. Dostoevsky's characters often face intense psychological and spiritual dilemmas, reflecting his own struggles and philosophical inquiries. His contributions to literature have earned him a place among the greatest novelists of all time, influencing countless writers and thinkers.-:-['The Gambler', 'Poor Folk', 'The Double']
Gabriel Garcia Marquez-:-Gabriel Garcia Marquez (1927-2014) was a Colombian novelist, short story writer, and journalist, widely regarded as one of the most significant authors of the 20th century. He is best known for pioneering the literary style known as magical realism, blending the fantastical with the ordinary in his works. Marquez's most famous novel, 'One Hundred Years of Solitude', is a landmark in world literature, depicting the multi-generational story of the Buendia family in the fictional town of Macondo. His other notable works include 'Love in the Time of Cholera' and 'Chronicle of a Death Foretold'. Marquez's storytelling is marked by rich, poetic language and deep political and social insights, earning him the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1982.-:-['One Hundred Years of Solitude', 'Love in the Time of Cholera', 'News of a Kidnapping', 'The General in His Labyrinth', 'The Handsomest Drowned Man in the World', 'Collected Novellas']
Cormac McCarthy-:-Cormac McCarthy (1933-2023) was an American novelist and playwright, known for his dark and often violent depictions of the American South and Southwest. His writing is characterized by its sparse, poetic prose and exploration of themes such as survival, morality, and the human condition. McCarthy's most acclaimed works include 'Blood Meridian', 'No Country for Old Men', and 'The Road', the latter of which won the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction in 2007. His bleak and unflinching narratives have cemented his reputation as a master of contemporary American literature, influencing a generation of writers and readers with his distinct voice and vision.-:-['The Road', 'Blood Meridian', 'No Country for Old Men']
F. Scott Fitzgerald-:-F. Scott Fitzgerald (1896-1940) was an American novelist and short story writer, widely regarded as one of the greatest American writers of the 20th century. He is best known for his novel 'The Great Gatsby', a quintessential portrayal of the Jazz Age and the American Dream. Born in Minnesota, Fitzgerald achieved early success with his debut novel 'This Side of Paradise'. His writing often explores themes of wealth, ambition, and societal decay. Despite his literary success, Fitzgerald struggled with personal issues, including alcoholism and financial instability. His works, particularly 'The Great Gatsby', remain enduring classics, offering sharp critiques of American society and the elusive pursuit of happiness.-:-['The Great Gatsby', 'Tender Is the Night', 'The Pat Hobby Stories', 'The Crack-Up']
Harper Lee-:-Harper Lee (1926-2016) was an American novelist best known for her Pulitzer Prize-winning novel 'To Kill a Mockingbird', a profound commentary on racial injustice and moral growth in the American South. Born in Monroeville, Alabama, Lee's own experiences growing up in the South deeply influenced her writing. Her portrayal of the fictional town of Maycomb and the character of Atticus Finch has become iconic in American literature. Despite the immense success of her first novel, Lee published only one other book during her lifetime, 'Go Set a Watchman', which was controversially released in 2015. Harper Lee's work continues to be celebrated for its powerful narrative and timeless themes of empathy and justice.-:-['To Kill a Mockingbird', 'Go Set a Watchman', 'Christmas to Me']
Toni Morrison-:-Toni Morrison (1931-2019) was an American novelist, essayist, and editor, celebrated for her richly textured and profound exploration of African American life and culture. Born in Ohio, Morrison's literary career took off with her debut novel 'The Bluest Eye'. She went on to write several critically acclaimed works, including 'Song of Solomon' and 'Beloved', the latter of which won the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction. Morrison's writing is known for its lyrical prose, deep psychological insight, and unflinching portrayal of the complexities of race, identity, and history. She was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1993, cementing her legacy as one of the most influential writers of her time.-:-['Beloved', 'Song of Solomon', 'The Bluest Eye', 'Home', 'God Help the Child', 'Tar Baby']
H.G. Wells-:-H.G. Wells (1866-1946) was an English writer and social commentator, often referred to as the "father of science fiction". His pioneering works in the genre include 'The War of the Worlds', 'The Time Machine', and 'The Invisible Man'. Born in Kent, Wells initially trained as a biologist before turning to writing. Wells was also a prolific writer of social and political commentary, advocating for progressive ideas and reforms. His innovative storytelling and visionary ideas have left a lasting impact on science fiction and literature as a whole.-:-['The War of the Worlds', 'The Time Machine', 'The Invisible Man', 'Ann Veronica']
John Steinbeck-:-John Steinbeck (1902-1968) was an American novelist and Nobel Prize winner, known for his poignant and socially conscious works depicting the plight of the working class. Born in California, Steinbeck's experiences in the agricultural heartland of America profoundly influenced his writing. His most famous novels include 'The Grapes of Wrath', which won the Pulitzer Prize, 'Of Mice and Men', and 'East of Eden'. Steinbeck's narrative style blends realism with a deep empathy for his characters, often highlighting themes of hardship, perseverance, and human dignity. His contributions to literature have established him as one of the foremost American writers of the 20th century.-:-['The Grapes of Wrath', 'Of Mice and Men', 'East of Eden', 'The Pearl']
J.D. Salinger-:-J.D. Salinger (1919-2010) was an American writer best known for his novel 'The Catcher in the Rye', a seminal work in modern American literature. Born in New York City, Salinger wrote extensively during and after World War II, gaining a reputation for his distinctive voice and introspective characters. 'The Catcher in the Rye', published in 1951, captures the adolescent alienation and loss of innocence through the eyes of its protagonist, Holden Caulfield. Salinger became famously reclusive after the success of his novel, retreating from public life but continuing to write privately. His influence on literature and popular culture remains significant, with his works continuing to resonate with readers across generations.-:-['The Catcher in the Rye', 'Nine Stories', 'Franny and Zooey', 'Raise High the Roof Beam, Carpenters and Seymour: An Introduction']

Tolstoy's legacy endures through his literary contributions and his advocacy for social and moral reform.-:-['War and Peace', 'Anna Karenina', 'The Death of Ivan Ilyich']

Dickens's keen observation of society and his ability to create memorable characters have made his novels enduring classics. Born into a poor family, Dickens's own experiences of hardship and child labor profoundly influenced his writing. His stories often combine humor, social commentary, and a strong moral message, reflecting his commitment to social reform and justice.-:-['A Christmas Carol', 'Great Expectations', 'A Tale of Two Cities', 'David Copperfield']
Jane Austen-:-Jane Austen (1775-1817) was an English novelist known for her keen observations of early 19th-century British society and her witty, romantic fiction. Her major works, including 'Pride and Prejudice', 'Sense and Sensibility', and 'Emma', explore themes of love, marriage, and social status. Austen's novels are celebrated for their sharp characterizations, engaging plots, and critique of the social norms of her time. Despite her relatively short life, Austen's literary legacy has endured, and her works remain popular for their timeless appeal and incisive commentary on human relationships and societal expectations.-:-['Pride and Prejudice', 'Sense and Sensibility', 'Emma', 'Northanger Abbey']
James Joyce-:-James Joyce (1882-1941) was an Irish writer and modernist, best known for his groundbreaking works 'Ulysses' and 'A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man'. Born in Dublin, Joyce's complex and innovative narrative techniques, such as stream of consciousness and rich, symbolic language, revolutionized 20th-century literature. 'Ulysses', in particular, is renowned for its detailed depiction of a single day in Dublin and its deep exploration of the inner lives of its characters. Joyce's work, while often challenging, has had a profound influence on modern literature, inspiring countless writers and continuing to be studied and celebrated for its literary genius.-:-['Ulysses', 'A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man', 'Finnegans Wake', 'Dubliners']

His major works, including 'The Metamorphosis', 'The Trial', and 'The Castle', are characterized by their nightmarish scenarios and profound existential questions. Kafka's unique narrative style and his exploration of the individual's struggle against oppressive systems have made him a defining figure in 20th-century literature.
